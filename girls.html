What’s new for 2025–26

A playing space of her own.

For the first time in the Surrey CVL, girls aged 7–13 can compete in proper, standalone girls’ divisions alongside the Mixed competition. Here’s what that means in practice.

Same sport, same league, different room. Girls in the U10 and U12 divisions play a full season of fixtures against other girls’ teams — with the same referees, same venues, same week-to-week rhythm as every other CVL division.

Why it matters. In mixed-gender basketball at these ages, boys are often physically larger and more confident ball-handlers. A girls’ division isn’t about separation — it’s about pace. Girls develop the game in an environment where they’re the ones driving play, calling shots, and leading their teams, without the dynamic a mixed court sometimes creates at younger ages.

Mixed is still there too. Plenty of girls play in the U10, U12 and older Mixed divisions — and always have. The girls’ divisions are an additional place to play, not a replacement.

Older age groups. U14, U16 and U18 players of any gender currently play in the Mixed division. Whether to add U14 Girls (and beyond) is an open question for the committee as participation grows.

A note on fairness

Following a recent committee decision, girls registered with a national league team are no longer permitted to play down in boys’ Mixed CVL divisions — including at playoffs. The rule supports a level playing field across the league.

National programmes

Girls’ basketball is growing everywhere.

Our work in Surrey sits alongside Basketball England and FIBA’s national campaigns to grow girls’ and women’s participation across every level of the game. If you want to go further than your local club, these are the places to look.

FIBA & Basketball England

Her World, Her Rules

FIBA’s flagship grassroots girls’ campaign, delivered in the UK by Basketball England. Female-only sessions for girls aged 6–15, now in its fourth year in the UK and running at over 40 clubs across England.

Parents & players

Your questions, answered.

My daughter has never played basketball before. Is that OK?

Absolutely. The U10 and U12 divisions are developmental by design — plenty of girls join having never picked up a basketball. Clubs train during the week and play on Sundays; new players are welcome throughout the year, subject to each club’s capacity.

What age does my daughter need to be?

The CVL’s youngest division is U10, meaning players who are under 10 at a cut-off date at the start of the season. In practice, most players in the U10 divisions are aged 7 to 9. U12 is for players who are under 12 at the cut-off — typically ages 9 to 11.

Specific age eligibility and cut-off dates are set in the CVL Handbook. Your club can confirm whether your daughter is eligible for a particular division.

Are the girls’ divisions all-girl teams, or mixed?

The U10 Girls and U12 Girls divisions are girls-only — every player on every team is a girl. This is a distinct competition from the U10 and U12 Mixed divisions, which are open to players of any gender.

Many clubs run both, so a girl can play in either — or both — depending on the club’s setup and what she wants.

What about older girls — U14 and above?

The U14, U16 and U18 divisions are currently Mixed only. Plenty of girls play in these divisions every season. Whether to introduce separate girls’ divisions at U14 and above is a question the SBA committee will review as participation grows.

At national league level, there are girls-only competitions (WNBL, WEABL) that run through older age groups — see the England Talent Pathway.

When and where are games played?

CVL games run on Sundays from late September through to May, at four venues across Surrey: Chessington Sports Centre, Three Rivers Academy Hersham, Tadworth Leisure & Community Centre, and St John the Baptist School Woking. Finals day is held at ACS International School Cobham.

Each club trains separately during the week — normally at their home venue or a local school hall. Training times and locations vary by club.

What do we need to bring? How much does it cost?

For games: basketball kit (shorts and top), basketball shoes (indoor court shoes with non-marking soles), a water bottle, and enthusiasm. Clubs provide match jerseys.

Costs vary by club and include a Basketball England player licence (paid to BE directly), plus a club subscription that covers training and match fees. Your chosen club will explain the breakdown when you contact them.

I want to help — can I volunteer even if I’ve never coached?

Yes. Clubs always need volunteers for table officials (scoring and timing at games), team management, and fixture logistics. Full training is provided for table officials — see the officiating pathway.

If coaching is where you’d like to go, Basketball England runs Level 1 and Level 2 coaching qualifications. Discounts are available through initiatives like All Girls Ambassadors if you’re specifically looking to support the women’s game.
